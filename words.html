Words

Book reviews, literary analysis, and reflections on the power of storytelling.

The Brothers Karamazov by Fyodor Dostoevsky

"Above all, don't lie to yourself. The man who lies to himself and listens to his own lie comes to a point that he cannot distinguish the truth within him, or around him, and so loses all respect for himself and for others." - Fyodor Dostoevsky

A passionate philosophical novel that enters deeply into the ethical debates of God, free will, and morality. It is a spiritual drama of moral struggles concerning faith, doubt, judgment, and reason, set against a modernizing Russia, with a plot which revolves around the subject of patricide.

East of Eden by John Steinbeck

"And now that you don't have to be perfect, you can be good." - John Steinbeck

Generational Family story that intertwines themes of Genesis into a large cast of characters in early 1900s America. It explores the complex nature of love, the pain of rejection, and the possibility of redemption through the power of choice.

Frankenstein by Mary Shelley

"Beware; for I am fearless, and therefore powerful." - Mary Shelley

A layered story about a man who seeks to create a living creature, and the misery that follows for both the creature and the man who made him. It serves as a cautionary tale about the dangers of unchecked ambition and the responsibility of creators to their creations.

Mere Christianity by C.S. Lewis

"If I find in myself desires which nothing in this world can satisfy, the only logical explanation is that I was made for another world." - C.S. Lewis

An introduction to the worldview held by Christians written for soldiers who were navigating a world that felt meaningless post WW1. Lewis argues for the logical validity of Christianity and explains its core tenets with clarity and wit.

The Count of Monte Cristo by Alexandre Dumas

"All human wisdom is contained in these two words - Wait and Hope." - Alexandre Dumas

A tale of vengeance, justice and Providence set in post-Napoleonic France. It follows Edmond Dantès as he escapes from prison, acquires a fortune, and sets about exacting revenge on those responsible for his imprisonment.

The Chronicles of Narnia by C.S. Lewis

"Courage, dear heart." - C.S. Lewis

This was the first book I read with my family, and will always be a nostalgic warm memory that I'm excited to share with my kids. It transports readers to a magical world where animals speak, magic is real, and good battles evil.

The Waves by Virginia Woolf

"I am rooted, but I flow." - Virginia Woolf

A novel that follows the course of one day sunrise to sunset alongside the inner impressions of 6 characters as they navigate life. The story is told through the inmost experiences of the characters, probing a layer deeper than feelings or thoughts.

The Death of Ivan Ilych by Leo Tolstoy

"Maybe I did not live as I ought to have done?" - Leo Tolstoy

A brief precise depiction of the life and death of one man. Tolstoy strips away the fluff of life removing the cushion of our existence. It forces the reader to confront the inevitability of death and the importance of living a meaningful life.

Zorba the Greek by Nikos Kazantzakis

"A man needs a little madness, or else... he never dares cut the rope and be free." - Nikos Kazantzakis

A novel about a young intellectual who tries to escape his bookish life with the help of the boisterous and mysterious Alexis Zorba.
